Submission Process: Journal of Law Studies The Journal of Law Studies welcomes submissions of original research articles, theoretical studies, empirical analyses, and methodological innovations that delve into various aspects of the legal domain. Our submission process is designed to be efficient, transparent, and supportive of authors throughout their publication journey. Below is an overview of our detailed submissions process:

1.Manuscript Preparation: Authors should ensure that their manuscripts adhere to the journal's guidelines, which can be found on our website. These guidelines encompass formatting requirements, citation style, and ethical considerations. Authors are provided with clear instructions on how to structure their articles and prepare them for review.

2.Online Submission: To submit a manuscript, authors should visit our online submission system, accessible via our website. The system guides authors through the submission process, step-by-step. Authors will be required to provide information such as the manuscript title, abstract, keywords, and the names and affiliations of all contributing authors.

3.Peer Review: Upon submission, manuscripts undergo a rigorous peer-review process. Our Editorial Board assigns the manuscript to experts in the legal field who evaluate its quality, originality, methodology, and significance. The peer-review process upholds the highest academic standards and assists authors in refining their work through constructive feedback.

4.Decision Notification: Following the completion of the peer-review process, authors will receive a decision notification via email. The decision can take various forms, including acceptance, minor revisions, major revisions, or rejection. In cases where revisions are requested, authors receive specific feedback to address before resubmitting their revised manuscript.

5.Revision and Resubmission: When revisions are required, authors are encouraged to thoughtfully consider the feedback provided by the reviewers and make the necessary revisions to their manuscript. The revised version should be submitted within the specified timeframe, accompanied by a detailed response addressing each reviewer's comments and explaining the changes made.

6.Copyediting and Production: Once a manuscript is accepted for publication, it undergoes copyediting to ensure adherence to style, grammar, and formatting guidelines. Authors may be requested to review and approve the edited version. The accepted articles are then prepared for online publication, including assignment of a digital object identifier (DOI) for proper identification and citation.

7.Online Publication: Accepted articles are published online, providing immediate accessibility to readers worldwide through the journal's website. The Online First section allows readers to access articles before the formal print or final online version of the journal is released, ensuring timely dissemination of research findings to the legal community.

8.Indexing and Archiving: The Journal of Law Studies endeavors to have its content indexed in reputable databases and repositories, enhancing the visibility and discoverability of published articles. This includes indexing in databases such as PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, and Google Scholar, among others. Articles are also archived to preserve the journal's content for future reference.

We invite researchers, scholars, and practitioners to submit their work to the Journal of Law Studies. By following our submissions process, authors have the opportunity to contribute to the advancement of knowledge in the legal field and engage in meaningful scholarly discussions. We look forward to receiving your submissions and joining you on the journey of expanding our understanding of law, its impact on society, and its evolution in the modern world.

Principles and Guidelines: Journal of Law I.Obligations of the Editorial Board 1.The Commission shall consider all research submitted for publication, and each shall be judged based on its merits regardless of race, religion, nationality, gender, seniority, or institutional affiliation of the author(s). However, the editor may immediately consider the research relationships without considering them with others previously or simultaneously submitted by the same author(s).

2.The sole responsibility for accepting or rejecting the research lies with the editor. The responsible and prudent practice of this requires that the editor seek advice from experts, who have been selected, regarding the credibility and reliability of the research submitted for publication. However, research may be rejected without external review if the editorial board deems it unsuitable for the journal. This rejection may be based on the failure of the research to fit the scope of the journal, to be of sufficient current or broad relevance, to provide sufficient depth to the content, or for other reasons.

3.The editor must consider the research submitted for publication at a reasonable speed. 4.Members of the editorial board should not disclose any information related to the research under consideration to anyone other than those from whom professional advice is sought.

5.The editor-in-chief and members of the editorial board may disclose or publish the titles of research papers and the names of the authors of works that have been accepted for publication, but the information may not be shared casually unless the author's express permission is obtained. If the editorial board decides that the research is not suitable for publication in the journal for scientific reasons, it may then transfer the research to an alternative journal.

6.The editorial board must respect the intellectual independence of authors. 7.Responsibility and editorial authority must be delegated to any research authored by a member of the board or by colleagues in the same institution wishing to be published in the journal and submitted by the editorial board to the expert committee to decide whether the research is valid. Consideration by the editorial board of such research in any way would constitute a conflict of interest and therefore considered inappropriate.

8.Unpublished information or explanations disclosed in the research submitted by the researcher should only be used with his written consent. However, if this information suggests that some of the author's research is unlikely to be financially rewarding, the editorial board can ethically stop the work.

9.If the submitted research is related to current or previous research of a member of the editorial board and in order not to create a conflict of interest, it is appropriate to inform the author about the editorial board's research and plans in this field.

10.If convincing evidence is provided to the editorial board that the main content or conclusions of research published in the journal are incorrect, the editorial board should facilitate the publication of an appropriate report indicating that error and, if possible, correct it.

11.The author may request the editorial board not to use certain experts in the consideration of the research. However, the editorial board may decide to use one or more of these reviewers, if the board feels that their opinions are important in the fair consideration of the research. This may be the case, for example, when research seriously disagrees with the previous work of a potential expert.

II.Moral obligations of the author Authors are expected to adhere to the following ethical guidelines set out below which may result in the application of sanctions by the editorial board, including but not limited to the suspension or revocation of publication privileges.

1.The author should provide accurate and complete details of the research carried out, avoiding deception altogether, including the data collected or used, as well as an objective discussion of the importance of the research. The data is identified as information collected or used to generate research conclusions. The research report and the data collected should contain sufficient detail and an indication of general sources of information to allow the trained professional to reproduce the observations.

2.The author must realize that journal space is a valuable resource created at a great cost. Therefore, the author is obliged to use it wisely and economically.

3.The author must cite those publications that have had the effect of determining the nature of the reported work and that will quickly direct the reader to the previous work necessary to understand the current investigation. Work citations that will not be referenced in the reported search should be minimized. The author is obliged to conduct a literature search to find and then cite original publications describing the closely related work. For critical materials used in the work, appropriate quotations should also be made to sources when they are provided by a non-author.

4.It is inappropriate for a researcher to submit the same research to more than one journal for publication unless the research is rejected or withdrawn from publication.

5.Authors are encouraged to recommend appropriate reviewers during the submission of the paper. However, they do not suggest reviewers who have an actual, perceived, or potential conflict of interest. Furthermore, authors should not suggest reviewers with whom they share a personal or professional relationship if there is a possibility that this relationship will introduce bias in the evaluation of the research.

6.The author must determine the source of all information quoted or displayed, except as known to all. Information that is published privately, such as in conversation, correspondence, or discussion with a third party, should not be used or reported in the author's work without the express permission of the investigator with whom the information originated. Information obtained at the Office of Secret Services, such as research arbitration or grant applications, should be treated similarly.

7.Empirical or theoretical study may sometimes justify criticism, and even extreme criticism, of the work of another scientist. Where appropriate, such criticism may be presented in published papers. However, personal criticism is by no means appropriate.

8.The co-authors of the paper should be all people who have made significant scientific contributions to the reported work and who share responsibility and accountability for the results. Authors must appropriately recognize the contributions of technical staff and data specialists. Other contributions should be indicated in a footnote or the "Acknowledgements" section.

9.The author must disclose to the editorial board and readers of the journal any actual or potential conflict of interest of any of the authors that may be related to the publication of the results contained in the research during submission. All authors must declare (1) sources of funding for the reported research, (2) the existence of any significant financial interest in companies or business entities that have a financial or other interest in the manuscript results, (3) any employment or other relationship (within the last three years) with entities that have a financial or other interest in the manuscript results (including but not limited to paid consultations, expert testimony, fees, patent interests, or Membership of advisory boards or committees). Financial interests are included in the published article. Authors must also disclose to the editorial board any non-financial possibility or perceived conflict of interest that, in their view, may affect the publication process.

10.Plagiarism is not acceptable in the journal. ( ) is committed to defining plagiarism as (appropriation of someone else's ideas, processes, results, or words) authors must not engage in plagiarism—verbatim or quasi-verbatim reproduction, or paraphrasing too close, to a text or results from another work. Authors must not engage in self-plagiarism (also known as duplicate publishing) – unacceptably replicating previously published text or results of the author without acknowledging the source. It is unacceptable for an author to include verbatim or semi-literal parts of his work, or to portray his previously published findings or methodology as new, without acknowledging the source.

11.Images in the search must be free from misleading manipulation. When they are cited or in data collection as part of the research, the researcher is prompted for an accurate description of how the images were obtained.

III.Ethical obligations of the Committee of Experts :

1.As much as research review is an essential step in the publication process, each expert is obliged to do a fair share of the review. 2.The selected expert who feels that he is not sufficiently qualified to judge the reported research should immediately return it to the editor. 3.Experts should judge the quality of research objectively, including empirical and theoretical data and explanations, with due regard to maintaining high scientific and literary standards. The reviewer must respect the intellectual independence of the authors.

4.The expert should be sensitive to the emergence of a conflict of interest when the research under review is closely related to the expert's ongoing or published work. In case of doubt, the expert must immediately re-examine without review, and inform the editorial board of the conflict of interest or bias.

5.The expert must not evaluate a manuscript that has been authored or co -authored by a person with whom he has a personal or professional relationship if the relationship would prejudice the judgment of the research.

6.Confidentiality throughout the editorial review process to allow for frank discussion and evaluation regarding the submitted scientific content. This confidentiality is independent of the final decision on the research and extends beyond (i.e., whether the research has been published or rejected). More specifically:

A.The expert must treat both the research and the data received from the journal, the audit report, and related correspondence as confidential. These documents and their contents should not be disclosed or discussed with any individual or organization.

B.In some special cases, an expert selected by the editorial board may seek advice or guidance from another expert to facilitate the review of the paper. In such cases, the expert must obtain the consent of the editorial board before sharing the contents of the manuscript and obtain editorial consent and disclose the identity of the other expert at that time.

C.An expert may publicly disclose that they have worked as an expert for the journal however, the expert should not identify himself or herself as a research expert in his or her own right.

D.In some special cases, the expert may wish to reveal his identity to the authors of the research for scientific reasons. Under these circumstances, the expert must obtain prior approval from the editorial board. The decision to disclose this information to authors is at the sole discretion of the Commission.

E.Experts should adequately explain and support their judgments so that editors and authors can understand the basis of their comments. Any statement that an observation, derivation, or argument has been previously reported must be accompanied by the relevant citation. Assertions not supported by experts (or authors in the appeal) are of little value and should be avoided.

F.The expert should be vigilant for authors' failure to cite relevant works by other scholars, bearing in mind that complaints that the reviewer's research has not been sufficiently cited may appear self-service.

G.The expert should draw the attention of the editorial board to any material similarity between the research under consideration and any published paper or any research submitted at the same time to another journal.

7.The expert must act immediately and submit a timely report. If the expert receives a research paper at a time when circumstances prevent immediate interest in the research, the research should be immediately returned to the editorial board or notify the editorial board of possible delays and suggest an alternative date.

8.Experts must not use or disclose unpublished information, arguments, or explanations contained in the research under consideration, except with the consent of the author.

9.A review of the research provided may sometimes justify criticism, and even severe criticism, from the expert. Where appropriate, such criticism may be presented in published papers. However, the personal criticism of the author is in no way appropriate.
